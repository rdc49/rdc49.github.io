About Me

Hi there, I'm Robb, a PhD student at the Institute of Astronomy in the University of Cambridge. I study the atmospheres of hot rocky planets: planets like Venus and Mercury that are too hot to sustain liquid water. In general, I'm interested in habitability and how hot rocky planets help us understand what can make a potentially habitable planet uninhabitable.

Research

Ozone on Venus

My recent work has focused on ozone in the atmospheres of Venus and Venus-like planets. The ozone layer in Venus' mesosphere is a mystery to planetary scientists; no one knows its origin. If a similar ozone layer exists on other worlds like Venus, then this would complicate the use of ozone as an identifier of a habitable Earth-like world. I've been using atmospheric models to determine where Venus' ozone is coming from, and if it might show up on other Venus-like worlds.

Magma Oceans on Sub-Neptunes

Currently, I am extending my research on hot rocky exoplanets to include the sub-Neptune regime, particularly focusing on 'Magma Worlds.' There has been recent interest in Hycean worlds—planets with vast liquid oceans and hydrogen-dominated atmospheres—and their potential to support life. However, distinguishing observationally between Hycean worlds and sub-Neptunes with molten surfaces, or 'Magma Worlds,' remains a critical challenge.
